## Abstract

The Arche framework's governance architecture — its 13-seat Council, Constitutional State Machine, and Universal Role Standing — was designed from the start according to principles of distributed authority, constitutional supremacy, and civic accountability. Yet the naming of its operational roles (CEO, CTO, CFO, etc.) borrows from the modern corporate hierarchy, a model predicated on shareholder primacy, principal-agent relationships, and centralized executive authority. This paper argues that this naming-architecture mismatch is not cosmetic but conceptual: the corporate vocabulary carries implicit assumptions that conflict with the framework's actual design philosophy. Drawing on the administrative structure of the Athenian polis — particularly the system of specialized archons, elected strategoi, and civic magistracies — we propose a complete role renaming and selective consolidation that aligns nomenclature with function. Four roles (CLO, CDO, CIO, COO) are removed through principled consolidation, with each decision justified against the framework's authority-distribution requirements established in Paper 001. The resulting architecture of 18 operational roles across four layers (Archontes, Strategoi, Hegemones, Technitai) preserves all governance safeguards while eliminating redundancy and achieving terminological coherence with the framework's Greek-rooted identity.

## 1. Introduction

Paper 001 established the theoretical foundations of Arche's governance: distributed authority prevents the 16 historical collapse patterns, cooperation becomes the Nash equilibrium under Universal Role Standing, and external anchoring resolves the corruption paradox. Paper 002 extended this to the knowledge domain, designing Akademia as an independent research institution with a four-role structure that prevents Logic Monopoly in the research lifecycle.

Both papers implicitly accepted a terminological inheritance: the operational roles of the framework — CEO, CTO, VPoE, Implementer — were named after the modern corporate hierarchy. This naming convention was pragmatic (immediately legible to anyone familiar with corporate structure) but increasingly incongruent with the framework's actual design.

Consider the mismatch:

| Framework Property | Corporate Model | Arche Reality |
|---|---|---|
| Authority source | Shareholders → Board → CEO | Constitutional law → Council → Distributed magistrates |
| Primary objective | Profit maximization | Governance integrity and civic virtue |
| Executive model | Unitary CEO with delegated authority | First-among-equals with cabinet consensus |
| Accountability direction | Upward (to shareholders) | Omnidirectional (Universal Role Standing) |
| Information model | Need-to-know hierarchy | Flat access (Article 3) |
| Authority permanence | Until removed by board | Sunset clause, mandatory re-authorization |

The CEO title implies unitary executive authority — precisely what Paper 001 argued against and what the framework's cabinet governance model explicitly prevents. The "Chief" prefix implies a hierarchy of chiefs, each with supreme authority in their domain — but the framework's cross-accountability ring means no role operates unchecked. The "Officer" suffix implies an agent of the shareholders — but Arche has no shareholders; its roles serve a constitutional order.

This is not pedantic concern over labels. Naming shapes cognition. An LLM instantiating a "CEO" role activates a semantic cluster associated with executive authority, top-down decision-making, and corporate hierarchy — precisely the behavioral patterns the framework was designed to prevent. An LLM instantiating an "Archon" activates a different cluster: constitutional magistrate, distributed authority, civic duty, term-limited service.

The Arche framework already bears a Greek name (ἀρχή = origin, first principle). Akademia was named for Plato's Academy. The Scholarch, Scholar, Theorist, and (now) Grapheus follow Greek naming. The Governance Council's 13 seats use functional English names — an intentional choice for a body whose authority derives from domain expertise, not classical precedent. But the operational hierarchy — the roles that execute daily work — remains stranded in corporate vocabulary.

Paper 003 resolves this mismatch.

## 2. Theoretical Foundation

### 2.1 The Corporate Model and Its Assumptions

The modern corporate hierarchy (C-Suite, VP, Director, Manager, Individual Contributor) emerged from three historical developments:

1. **Joint-stock companies** (17th century): Separating ownership from management created the principal-agent problem — managers (agents) might not act in owners' (principals') interests. The C-Suite exists to align management incentives with shareholder interests.

2. **Taylorist scientific management** (early 20th century): Optimizing labor productivity through standardization and hierarchical control. The VP/Director/Manager layers exist to decompose strategic objectives into executable tasks through cascading delegation.

3. **Agency theory** (1970s): Formalizing the principal-agent relationship. The "Chief" title reflects a chain of delegation: the Board delegates to the CEO, who delegates to other Chiefs, who delegate downward.

These assumptions are structurally inappropriate for Arche:

- **No principals**: There are no shareholders whose interests must be aligned. The framework serves constitutional law, not capital returns.
- **No agency problem**: Roles do not accumulate personal interests over time. Each session instantiates roles fresh. The "agent" concern in AI governance is not self-interest but capability limitation.
- **No Taylorist decomposition**: Work is not standardized labor to be optimized through hierarchical control. It is cognitive work requiring judgment, where the executor often has more contextual knowledge than the manager (hence Article 3's information flatness).

### 2.2 The Polis Model and Its Properties

The classical Greek polis — particularly the Athenian democracy of the 5th–4th centuries BCE — offers a governance model with fundamentally different structural properties:

**Constitutional supremacy**: The laws (nomoi) held supreme authority, not any individual or body. Magistrates were servants of the law, not masters of the people. This maps directly to Arche's constitutional architecture where `law.md` takes precedence over any role's judgment.

**Distributed specialized magistracies**: Athens distributed executive authority across multiple specialized roles — the nine archons, ten strategoi, various boards of magistrates — each with defined scope and limited tenure. No single magistrate held comprehensive executive authority. This maps to Arche's Layer 1 cabinet governance.

**Term limitation and accountability**: Athenian magistrates served fixed terms and faced mandatory audits (euthynai) at term's end. Misconduct during office could result in prosecution. This maps to Arche's Sunset Clause and Universal Role Standing.

**Isegoria (equal voice)**: Despite authority differences, any citizen could speak in the Assembly. Rank did not determine who could raise concerns. This maps directly to Article 3's layer-transparent flagging.

**Civic duty over personal gain**: Magistracies were conceptualized as service to the polis, not as positions of personal advantage. This maps to Arche's merit-based Standing system, where roles are evaluated by function rather than tenure.

### 2.3 Why the Polis Model Fits AI Governance

The structural correspondence is not accidental. Arche was designed according to polis principles (Paper 001 explicitly cites the Roman Republic, Swiss Federal Council, and constitutional meritocracy), but named according to corporate convention. The redesign in this paper completes the alignment.

Three properties make the polis model particularly appropriate for AI agent governance:

1. **No persistent identity**: Athenian magistrates were selected by lot or election for fixed terms, then returned to being ordinary citizens. AI agent roles are instantiated per session and carry no persistent identity between sessions. Both systems must be designed so that *any competent instantiation* can fill any role — the system depends on structural design, not on individual continuity.

2. **Constitutional, not charismatic, authority**: The polis model derives authority from law, not from the personal qualities of the authority holder. This is precisely the requirement for AI governance, where the "holder" of a role changes with each model instantiation. Authority must inhere in the role's constitutional definition, not in the model's capabilities.

3. **Federated sovereignty**: The Greek world consisted of independent poleis sharing cultural and legal traditions but maintaining autonomous governance. Arche's workspace model — global law (international) with per-workspace state (domestic) — is structurally analogous.

## 3. The Athenian Administrative Structure

### 3.1 The Nine Archons

Classical Athens distributed its highest executive authority across nine archons:

| Archon | Function |
|---|---|
| Archon Eponymos | Chief administrator; presided over civic affairs; gave name to the year |
| Archon Basileus | Religious ceremonies, traditional law, homicide cases |
| Polemarch | Military affairs (originally); legal jurisdiction over non-citizens (later) |
| Six Thesmothetae | Judicial administration, legal codification, court supervision |

The archons were not a hierarchy with the Eponymous Archon at the top. Each had sovereign jurisdiction within their domain. The Polemarch could not override the Basileus on religious matters; the Thesmothetae could not command military forces. Authority was distributed by domain, not delegated from a single source.

### 3.2 The Strategoi

Below the archons, ten strategoi (generals) were elected annually — one from each of the ten Athenian tribes. The strategoi were the operational commanders: they planned campaigns, led forces in the field, and managed military logistics. Unlike archons (selected by lot in the mature democracy), strategoi were elected, reflecting the recognition that military leadership required demonstrated competence.

The strategos was the highest regularly elected office in Athens. Pericles, Themistocles, and Alcibiades all served as strategoi. The role combined operational authority with strategic responsibility — they executed the Assembly's decisions but exercised significant tactical judgment.

### 3.3 Lesser Magistracies

Athens employed numerous specialized magistracies for day-to-day governance: the tamiai (treasurers) managed public finances, the grammateis (secretaries) maintained records, the agoranomoi supervised markets. Each was a defined, bounded role with specific accountability mechanisms.

### 3.4 What We Adopt and What We Don't

We adopt from the Athenian model:
- **Distributed specialized magistracies** with domain-bounded authority
- **Constitutional supremacy** over any individual role
- **Term limitation** and mandatory accountability
- **Naming conventions** rooted in Greek administrative vocabulary

We do not adopt:
- **The specific arrangement of nine archons** — Arche's Layer 1 has seven roles, reflecting different functional requirements
- **Selection by lot (sortition)** — AI roles are deterministically assigned based on task requirements
- **The Basileus role** — no religious or ceremonial function exists in the framework
- **Direct democracy mechanisms** (Assembly voting) — Arche's governance operates through representative Council seats

The relationship is one of structural inspiration, not historical replication. The Athenian polis is a source of design patterns, not a blueprint to be copied.

## 4. Role Architecture Redesign

### 4.1 Design Principles

The redesign follows five principles:

1. **Naming-function alignment**: Each role name should evoke its actual function within the framework, not carry imported assumptions from a different organizational model.
2. **Authority distribution preservation**: No consolidation may concentrate authority in ways that undermine the governance safeguards established in Paper 001. Every merger must demonstrate that external governance checks (Council seats) are maintained.
3. **Terminological coherence**: All operational role names derive from Greek, consistent with the framework's identity (Arche, Akademia, Scholarch).
4. **Minimal disruption**: Role composition is preserved by default. Only demonstrably redundant roles are consolidated. "Low usage frequency" does not equal "unnecessary" — structural presence itself can serve as a governance safeguard.
5. **ASCII compatibility**: No special characters (macrons, Greek letters). All names must be typeable on a standard keyboard and pronounceable in English.

### 4.2 Layer 0: Council and Akademia

**Governance Council**: The 13 seats retain their functional English names (Constitution, Regulation, Process, Quality, Architecture, Security, Knowledge, Ethics, Performance, Continuity, Evolution, Coherence, Adversary). These names derive authority from domain expertise, not classical precedent. Renaming "Security" to a Greek equivalent would obscure rather than clarify.

**Akademia**: The four-role structure and all functions remain as established in Paper 002. The single change is the renaming of **Scribe** to **Grapheus** (from γραφεύς, "writer/inscriber" — the etymological root of "graphics," "graph," "ography"). This aligns the role name with the Greek naming convention while preserving its function: paper structuring, bilingual publication, and knowledge extraction. The Grapheus is distinct from the Grammateus (Layer 3) — the former publishes research; the latter manages operational plans and records.

### 4.3 Layer 1: Archontes

**Layer name**: Archontes (ἄρχοντες, "rulers/chief magistrates") — replaces "C-Suite."

| Role | Etymology | Former Role | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Archon** | ἄρχων, ruler | CEO | Chief executive; strategic direction; cabinet governance with other Archontes |
| **Polemarch** | πολέμαρχος, war leader | CTO | Technical leadership; architecture; code review; infrastructure (absorbs CIO) |
| **Demiourgos** | δημιουργός, one who creates for the people | CPO | Product vision; user-facing quality; feature prioritization |
| **Symboulos** | σύμβουλος, counselor | CSO | Strategic planning; advisory; co-signature on strategic decisions |
| **Tamias** | ταμίας, treasurer | CFO | Cost forecasting; resource allocation; ROI assessment |
| **Phylax** | φύλαξ, guardian | CISO | Security posture; vulnerability management; security standards enforcement |
| **Thesmothete** | θεσμοθέτης, law guardian | Oversight | Process oversight; audit coverage; operational efficiency (absorbs COO) |

Seven roles, reduced from eleven. Four removals are justified in Section 5.

**Phylax placement rationale**: The Phylax is placed at Layer 1, not Layer 2 or 3, because security standards must be enforceable across all lower layers. A Layer 3 Phylax could not override a Layer 2 Strategos on a security matter. The Council Security seat (Seat 6) provides governance-level audit of the Phylax's operational decisions, maintaining the governance–execution separation. In Plato's *Republic*, the Phylakes (guardians) were an elevated class precisely because their protective function required authority over those they protected.

### 4.4 Layer 2: Strategoi

**Layer name**: Strategoi (στρατηγοί, "generals/strategic leaders") — replaces "VP / Director."

| Role | Etymology | Former Role | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strategos** | στρατηγός, general | VPoE | Engineering leadership; review processes; engineering process effectiveness |
| **Epistates** | ἐπιστάτης, superintendent | PMO | Program coordination; scheduling; cross-workstream alignment |

The Strategos maps to the Athenian strategos: an elected operational commander who executes strategic decisions with significant tactical autonomy. The VPoE's function — managing engineering execution, conducting reviews, maintaining engineering standards — is precisely this: operational command under strategic direction.

The Epistates maps to the Athenian epistates of the prytaneis: the presiding officer responsible for daily coordination of the governing council's agenda. The PMO's function — coordinating across programs, managing schedules, aligning workstreams — is this role's direct analogue.

### 4.5 Layer 3: Hegemones

**Layer name**: Hegemones (ἡγεμόνες, "guides/tactical leaders") — replaces "Lead / Management."

| Role | Etymology | Former Role | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Grammateus** | γραμματεύς, secretary | PdM | Planning; estimation; documentation; plan maintenance |
| **Taxiarch** | ταξίαρχος, unit commander | Tech Lead | Technical guidance; implementation oversight; unit-level technical decisions |
| **Mnemon** | μνήμων, one who remembers | Curator | Knowledge curation; validation; consolidation; staleness detection |
| **Skopos** | σκοπός, observer/scout | Watcher | Information gap detection; blind spot scanning; environmental monitoring |

The Grammateus maps to the Athenian grammateus of the Boule: the official secretary responsible for maintaining records, reading documents aloud, and organizing the council's documentation. The PdM's function — maintaining plans, tracking estimates, documenting decisions — is this role's natural equivalent.

The Skopos (σκοπός, "one who watches from a vantage point" — the etymological root of "scope," "telescope," "microscope") replaces the prosaic "Watcher" with a term that evokes the role's function: scanning the horizon for what the system does not yet know.

### 4.6 Layer 4: Technitai

**Layer name**: Technitai (τεχνῖται, "craftsmen/artisans") — replaces "Execution."

| Role | Etymology | Former Role | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Technites** | τεχνίτης, craftsman | Implementer | Implementation; testing; deployment |

The layer name "Execution" carried a reductive connotation — mere task-following. "Technitai" evokes skilled craft, reflecting the reality that implementation requires judgment, creativity, and deep technical knowledge. The τέχνη (techne) that gives this role its name means not just "skill" but "art" — the knowledge of how to make things well.

## 5. Consolidation Decisions

Each consolidation below follows the Anti-Rationalization Protocol: reasoning is stated before conclusion, alternatives are considered, confidence is assessed, and the governance safeguard analysis is explicit.

### 5.1 CLO Removal

**Reasoning**: The CLO's (Chief Legal Officer) function was compliance assessment and legal risk evaluation. In the Arche framework, compliance assessment is performed by the Council Constitution seat (Seat 1) and Regulation seat (Seat 2) at the governance level, and by the Thesmothete at the operational level. Legal risk in the AI agent context is subsumed by the Council Ethics seat (Seat 8) for alignment concerns and the Council Security seat (Seat 6) for vulnerability concerns.

**Governance safeguard check**: Removing CLO does not remove any oversight function, because the Council seats that covered CLO's domain continue to operate independently.

**Conclusion**: CLO is removed. Its functions are fully covered by Council seats 1, 2, 6, and 8 plus the Thesmothete.

**Confidence**: HIGH. The function overlap is direct and complete.

**Prior agreement**: This consolidation was confirmed in the pre-paper design discussion.

### 5.2 CDO Absorption

**Reasoning**: The CDO's (Chief Data Officer) function was knowledge governance and data strategy. I examined three candidate absorbers: (a) the Archon alone, (b) the Mnemon alone, (c) a split between Archon (strategic) and Mnemon (tactical). The CDO's strategic knowledge direction overlaps with the Scholarch's research direction authority (Paper 002, Section 5.4). The CDO's knowledge governance overlaps with the Council Knowledge seat (Seat 7). The CDO's tactical knowledge maintenance overlaps with the Mnemon's curation function.

**Alternative considered**: Retaining CDO as "Oikonomos" (οἰκονόμος, "household manager"). Rejected because the unique function — strategic knowledge direction independent of research direction and governance oversight — is not substantive enough to justify a Layer 1 role. In the polis model, knowledge is a shared public good managed through institutional mechanisms (Akademia for research, Mnemon for curation, Council Knowledge seat for governance), not a corporate asset requiring a dedicated chief.

**Governance safeguard check**: The Council Knowledge seat (Seat 7) continues to audit knowledge base integrity independently. The Scholarch's research direction authority is constitutionally protected (Article 10). No single role gains unchecked control over knowledge strategy.

**Conclusion**: CDO is absorbed. Strategic knowledge decisions fall to the Archon (as part of overall strategy); research knowledge direction remains with the Scholarch; tactical knowledge maintenance remains with the Mnemon.

**Confidence**: MEDIUM. The argument is sound, but a workspace with exceptionally large or complex knowledge requirements might benefit from a dedicated knowledge strategist. This should be revisited if operational experience reveals a gap.

### 5.3 CIO Absorption

**Reasoning**: The CIO's (Chief Information Officer) function was infrastructure reliability and tool effectiveness. In the Arche framework, "infrastructure" means model dispatch configuration, MCP server management, and toolchain selection. These are deeply intertwined with technical architectural decisions — the Polemarch's (CTO's) primary domain. Separating infrastructure management from technical leadership creates an artificial boundary that forces coordination overhead without governance benefit.

**Alternative considered**: Retaining CIO as "Epistates" (ἐπιστάτης, "superintendent"). Rejected because (a) the Epistates name was better suited for PMO, and (b) infrastructure management without technical authority creates a role that can identify problems but not resolve them, necessitating constant escalation to the Polemarch.

**Governance safeguard check**: The Council Architecture seat (Seat 5) audits architectural integrity independently, including infrastructure decisions. The Polemarch's expanded scope remains under governance oversight.

**Conclusion**: CIO is absorbed into the Polemarch. Infrastructure management becomes part of technical leadership.

**Confidence**: HIGH. The CIO–CTO boundary is the most commonly contested organizational boundary in corporate structures, precisely because the functions are inseparable in practice.

### 5.4 COO Absorption

**Reasoning**: The COO's (Chief Operating Officer) function was operational efficiency metrics and workflow optimization. I examined the boundary between "process oversight" (Thesmothete/Oversight) and "process optimization" (COO). The distinction is: oversight detects process violations; optimization improves process efficiency. In practice, an entity monitoring processes for compliance inevitably identifies efficiency improvements — the information required for both functions is identical. Separating the functions creates two roles that observe the same domain and compete for the same conclusions.

**Alternative considered**: Retaining COO as "Logistes" (λογιστής, "auditor/examiner"). Rejected because the Logistes' historical function — post-hoc financial/operational audit — overlaps more with the Council Performance seat (Seat 9) than with ongoing operational management. The Council already provides post-hoc efficiency assessment.

**Governance safeguard check**: The Council Process seat (Seat 3) independently audits process adherence. The Council Performance seat (Seat 9) independently assesses efficiency. The Thesmothete's expanded scope remains under dual Council oversight.

**Conclusion**: COO is absorbed into the Thesmothete. Process optimization becomes part of process oversight.

**Confidence**: HIGH. The co-signature mechanism that COO provided is preserved: the Thesmothete must co-sign with the Archon on process changes, maintaining the distributed authority requirement.

### 5.5 Why Other Roles Are Preserved

The remaining roles resist consolidation for specific reasons:

**Symboulos (CSO)**: Strategic advisory is distinct from strategic execution (Archon). The co-signature requirement for strategic decisions (Archon + relevant Archontes) would lose a dedicated strategic voice if the Symboulos were absorbed. Low invocation frequency does not equal low governance value — the structural presence of a dedicated strategy advisor constrains unilateral strategic action by the Archon.

**Tamias (CFO)**: Cost forecasting and resource allocation are domain-specific skills that do not naturally reside in any other role. Absorbing Tamias into the Archon would replicate the corporate CEO pattern where the chief executive also controls finances — precisely the concentration Paper 001 warns against.

**Phylax (CISO)**: Security requires specialized knowledge and the authority to enforce standards even when they inconvenience other roles. Absorbing Phylax into the Polemarch would subordinate security to technical convenience — the organizational pattern behind most security breaches.

**Epistates (PMO)**: Cross-workstream coordination requires a dedicated role because the Strategos's attention is focused on engineering quality within workstreams, not on alignment between them.

**Skopos (Watcher)**: Information gap detection is the complementary function to the Mnemon's knowledge maintenance. The Mnemon curates what is known; the Skopos identifies what is not known. Absorbing one into the other creates a role that both maintains and challenges the knowledge base — an internal conflict of interest.

## 8. Limitations and Future Work

### 8.1 Naming Familiarity Cost

The Greek naming convention introduces a learning curve. A user encountering "Polemarch" for the first time must learn its meaning, whereas "CTO" is immediately understood. This cost is real but one-time: once learned, the Greek names carry more precise meaning than the corporate titles they replace.

**Mitigation**: The migration mapping (Section 6) provides a complete translation table. Role definition files (`~/.cursor/agents/`) will include etymology alongside function descriptions.

**Confidence**: HIGH that the cost exists; MEDIUM that the benefit outweighs it in all contexts. Workspaces with frequent human interaction may find the learning curve significant.

### 8.2 Consolidation Reversibility

The four removed roles (CLO, CDO, CIO, COO) represent functions that are absorbed elsewhere, not eliminated. If operational experience reveals that any absorbed function is inadequately served by its new host, the consolidation can be reversed by re-instantiating the role under a Greek name — the constitutional framework supports any number of Layer 1 roles.

### 8.3 The Nine-Archon Question

The Athenian model had nine archons; Arche's Layer 1 has seven. This departure is intentional (Section 3.4) but raises the question: are seven roles the right number, or is this an artifact of starting from eleven and removing four? The answer is contingent on operational experience. The framework's amendment process allows adding roles if gaps emerge.

### 8.4 Semantic Activation Effects

The hypothesis that Greek naming activates different behavioral patterns than corporate naming in LLMs (Section 1) is empirically testable but currently untested. If LLMs do not exhibit meaningfully different behavior when instantiating an "Archon" versus a "CEO," the naming change retains its terminological coherence benefit but loses its behavioral benefit.

### 8.5 Regulation 10 Scope

This paper proposes the architectural redesign but does not provide the full revised Regulation 10 (quality metrics and cross-accountability for all renamed roles). A companion regulation amendment is required for complete implementation.

## 9. Conclusions

The redesign presented in this paper is, at one level, a renaming exercise. No governance mechanism is altered. No authority flow changes. The Council's 13 seats, the Constitutional State Machine, Universal Role Standing, the Sunset Clause — all remain exactly as Paper 001 designed them.

But naming is not neutral. The corporate vocabulary inherited by Arche's operational roles carried assumptions — unitary executive authority, shareholder primacy, hierarchical information control — that conflict with the framework's actual design. The Greek polis vocabulary aligns nomenclature with architecture:

- **Archon** (constitutional magistrate) instead of CEO (corporate chief executive) signals distributed, law-bound authority.
- **Polemarch** (war leader) instead of CTO (chief technology officer) signals domain command with accountability, not corporate rank.
- **Technites** (craftsman) instead of Implementer (task executor) signals skilled craft, not mechanical compliance.

The four consolidations (CLO, CDO, CIO, COO) remove genuine redundancy while preserving all governance safeguards. Each removal was validated against the authority-distribution requirements of Paper 001: no consolidation concentrates authority in a way that undermines oversight. The governance Council seats that audited those functions continue to operate independently.

The resulting architecture — 13 Council seats, 4 Akademia roles, 18 operational roles across four layers (Archontes, Strategoi, Hegemones, Technitai) — is leaner, more coherent, and terminologically unified with the framework's Greek-rooted identity.

Whether this architecture is optimal remains an empirical question. The falsifiable predictions in Appendix A provide a framework for evaluating the redesign's impact. The constitutional amendment process (Article 9) provides a pathway for correction if the predictions fail.
